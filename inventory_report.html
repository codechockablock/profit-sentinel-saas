Missing Cost Data critical

üìñ Definition

Missing cost data means the 'Cost' field is blank or null for a product. Without this data, you cannot calculate Cost of Goods Sold, gross profit, gross margin, inventory valuation, or any meaningful financial metric for this item. This is a critical data quality issue affecting your entire financial reporting.

‚öôÔ∏è How This Occurs

‚Ä¢ Incomplete product setup: Cost field skipped during item creation ‚Ä¢ Vendor pricing not entered: Received goods without logging cost ‚Ä¢ Data migration issues: Cost field not mapped during system change ‚Ä¢ Free/promotional items: Assumption that free items don't need cost ‚Ä¢ Price changes: Old cost deleted, new cost not entered ‚Ä¢ System errors: Cost field accidentally cleared

üí∞ COGS Impact

COGS is COMPLETELY UNKNOWN for these items. Your P&L's cost of goods sold line is understated by the entire cost value of sales from these items. If they represent 10% of sales, your reported COGS could be understated by 10% or more.

üìà Gross Profit Impact

Gross profit is OVERSTATED by the missing COGS amount. You're recording revenue but no cost, making profit appear higher than reality. This is a material misstatement if the affected items represent significant sales volume.

üìä Gross Margin Impact

Gross margin percentage is UNRELIABLE and likely overstated. You're dividing profit by revenue, but the profit number excludes costs. Actual margin could be significantly lower than reported.

‚ö†Ô∏è Additional Business Risks

  • Financial statements are materially misstated
  • Tax liability may be incorrect (underreported COGS = overpaid taxes)
  • Inventory valuation wrong: balance sheet overstated or understated
  • Pricing decisions made without knowing true costs
  • Cannot calculate margin by item, category, or vendor
  • Audit findings possible: material weakness in controls

High-Value Dead Stock critical

üìñ Definition

High-value dead stock are items that meet dead stock criteria (no sales 12+ months) AND have significant inventory value on hand (typically $1,000+). These represent major working capital at risk of write-off and deserve urgent executive attention.

‚öôÔ∏è How This Occurs

‚Ä¢ Large initial orders of items that didn't sell as expected ‚Ä¢ Bulk discount purchases that turned out to be poor decisions ‚Ä¢ Seasonal items that didn't clear and were held over ‚Ä¢ Product line discontinuation without liquidation ‚Ä¢ Special orders for projects that got cancelled ‚Ä¢ Vendor closeout buys that didn't move

üí∞ COGS Impact

Significant COGS will need to be recognized as inventory write-down or write-off. A $10,000 high-value dead stock position may require a $10,000 expense recognition when properly accounted for under GAAP.

üìà Gross Profit Impact

Large negative impact to gross profit when write-down is recorded. A single high-value dead stock item could wipe out a week's worth of gross profit.

üìä Gross Margin Impact

When write-down hits P&L, gross margin drops significantly for that period. May cause missed profit targets or covenant issues.

‚ö†Ô∏è Additional Business Risks

  • Major cash tied up with no return pathway
  • Balance sheet overstated until write-down recorded
  • Audit finding likely if not properly reserved
  • Opportunity cost: this capital could be working elsewhere
  • May need to pay for disposal if items can't be sold or donated

Low Stock / Reorder Point Alert high

üìñ Definition

Low stock indicates that inventory levels have fallen below the safety threshold needed to maintain sales while awaiting replenishment. For hardware stores, this means popular items that contractors and DIYers rely on are at risk of stocking out, potentially sending customers to competitors.

‚öôÔ∏è How This Occurs

‚Ä¢ Demand spike: Unexpected project or contractor buying more than usual ‚Ä¢ Reorder delays: Purchase order not placed on time ‚Ä¢ Supplier issues: Extended lead times or backorders from distributors ‚Ä¢ Forecasting errors: Underestimating seasonal or project-based demand ‚Ä¢ Cash constraints: Delaying orders due to cash flow ‚Ä¢ System failures: Reorder alerts not triggering properly

üí∞ COGS Impact

COGS itself isn't directly affected, but the OPPORTUNITY COST of lost sales is significant. Every stockout represents revenue that would have generated COGS expense and gross profit that now goes to competitors instead.

üìà Gross Profit Impact

Gross profit is LOST for every sale that can't be fulfilled. If you stock out of a $25 item with 40% margin that normally sells 20/week, that's $200 in lost gross profit per week - often more than the cost of carrying safety stock.

üìä Gross Margin Impact

While margin percentage isn't affected, TOTAL margin dollars are reduced. Additionally, rush shipping to expedite restock orders often comes at premium cost, further eroding margins on subsequent sales.

‚ö†Ô∏è Additional Business Risks

  • Lost sales: Direct revenue loss when customers can't buy
  • Customer defection: Contractors may switch to competitor permanently
  • Project delays: Customer projects stalled waiting for your item
  • Rush order costs: Expedited shipping is expensive
  • Reputation damage: Word spreads about unreliable stock
  • Lost basket: Customer who can't find one item may buy nothing

Dead Stock / Obsolete Inventory high

üìñ Definition

Dead stock refers to inventory items that have had no sales activity for an extended period (typically 12+ months). In hardware stores, this often includes discontinued product lines, seasonal items that didn't sell, or specialty items ordered for customers who never picked them up. These items are sitting on shelves, tying up capital, and likely losing value through obsolescence.

‚öôÔ∏è How This Occurs

‚Ä¢ Over-ordering: Purchasing too much of slow-selling items or vendor minimums ‚Ä¢ Product discontinuation: Manufacturers stopped making item, but you still have stock ‚Ä¢ Trend changes: Technology or building code changes made items obsolete ‚Ä¢ Seasonality misjudgment: Ordering seasonal items that didn't sell during season ‚Ä¢ Special orders not picked up: Customer-ordered items never retrieved ‚Ä¢ Planogram changes: Items removed from active selling areas but still in inventory ‚Ä¢ Vendor closeouts: Buying deeply discounted items that don't move

üí∞ COGS Impact

COGS is UNDERSTATED in the current period because these items haven't sold. However, under proper accounting (GAAP/IFRS), dead stock should be written down to Net Realizable Value (NRV). If NRV is lower than cost (often $0 for truly obsolete items), an inventory write-down expense should be recognized, increasing COGS or being recorded separately.

üìà Gross Profit Impact

Gross profit is OVERSTATED if dead stock hasn't been written down. The balance sheet shows inventory at historical cost when its true recoverable value is much lower. When you eventually liquidate at clearance prices or throw away the inventory, you'll recognize a large loss that should have been accrued gradually.

üìä Gross Margin Impact

Gross margins are MISLEADING because they're calculated against sales that exclude these problematic items. Your overall inventory investment's return is actually lower than margins suggest. A hardware store showing 35% margin may have true return on inventory investment of only 25% when dead stock losses are factored in.

‚ö†Ô∏è Additional Business Risks

  • Working capital locked: Cash tied up in unsellable inventory
  • Storage costs: Shelf space and warehouse space occupied unproductively
  • Opportunity cost: Space and capital not available for faster-moving items
  • Physical deterioration: Hardware items can rust, packaging degrades
  • Safety hazards: Old chemicals, paints may become hazardous
  • Year-end write-offs: Large, unexpected write-downs at fiscal year-end
  • Investor/lender concerns: Overstated inventory affects financial ratios

üìñ Definition

High margin leak identifies items where the actual gross margin is significantly below the expected or target margin for that category. In hardware retail, if your target is 35% and items are selling at 15%, that's a margin leak that compounds with every sale.

‚öôÔ∏è How This Occurs

‚Ä¢ Pricing not updated after cost increase from vendor ‚Ä¢ Competitive pressure: matching low prices without considering margin ‚Ä¢ Promotion pricing left in place too long ‚Ä¢ Cost entered incorrectly (too high) making margin appear low ‚Ä¢ Retail price entered incorrectly (too low) ‚Ä¢ Category margin targets not set appropriately

üí∞ COGS Impact

COGS itself is accurate, but the RATIO of COGS to revenue is too high. Each sale generates less gross profit than it should, compounding over time into significant profit shortfall.

üìà Gross Profit Impact

Gross profit is LOWER THAN EXPECTED for each sale. If you sell 100 units at 15% margin instead of 35% margin on a $20 item, you've lost $4 per unit = $400 in gross profit that should have been earned.

üìä Gross Margin Impact

Blended gross margin is dragged down by these items. A few high-volume low-margin items can significantly impact overall margin even if most items are priced correctly.

  • Profit shortfall compounds with volume
  • Working harder for less profit

Zero Cost with Positive Quantity high

üìñ Definition

This flags items where the cost field is explicitly zero (not blank) but inventory exists. While some items legitimately have zero cost (free samples, promotional items, damaged items written off), most inventory has acquisition costs. A $0 cost is suspicious and usually indicates a data entry error or system issue.

‚öôÔ∏è How This Occurs

‚Ä¢ Data entry error: Accidentally entering 0 instead of actual cost ‚Ä¢ Placeholder value: Using 0 as placeholder that was never updated ‚Ä¢ Free samples/promos: Legitimately free items (should be documented) ‚Ä¢ Received as vendor credit: Items received without new cost entered ‚Ä¢ System default: Some POS systems default to 0 if not specified ‚Ä¢ Price file issue: Vendor cost file had errors

üí∞ COGS Impact

COGS is recorded as $0 when these items sell, even though you likely paid for them. This understates COGS. If you sell $5,000 of zero-cost items that actually cost $3,000, your COGS is understated by $3,000.

üìà Gross Profit Impact

Gross profit is overstated by the missing cost amount. Sales of these items appear 100% profitable when they're not. This inflates overall gross profit figures.

üìä Gross Margin Impact

These items show 100% gross margin (Revenue - $0 = 100% profit), which is almost never accurate for purchased inventory. This artificially inflates blended margin calculations and category margin analysis.

‚ö†Ô∏è Additional Business Risks

  • Tax implications: overstating profit, potentially overpaying taxes
  • Pricing errors: thinking you can price lower because 'no cost'
  • Inventory valuation: balance sheet shows $0 for real assets
  • Insurance: if inventory is lost, claim would be for $0

Shrinkage Pattern high

üìñ Definition

Shrinkage pattern identifies items that have characteristics commonly associated with inventory shrinkage (theft, damage, or administrative errors): high inventory value, low or no sales, and often below-expected margin. These items warrant investigation for potential loss.

‚öôÔ∏è How This Occurs

‚Ä¢ Employee theft: High-value items taken without ringing sale ‚Ä¢ Customer theft: Shoplifting of desirable items ‚Ä¢ Receiving fraud: Items received but diverted before shelving ‚Ä¢ Damaged and not recorded: Items damaged but not written off ‚Ä¢ Mis-picks: Wrong items shipped, returns not processed ‚Ä¢ Administrative errors: Data entry mistakes in receiving/adjustments

üí∞ COGS Impact

COGS is understated because items that should have flowed through as sold (or written off) are sitting in inventory. The cost of shrinkage isn't recognized until a physical inventory reveals the loss.

üìà Gross Profit Impact

Gross profit is overstated by the amount of unrecognized shrinkage. When physical count reveals shortages, a write-down will reduce profit for that period.

üìä Gross Margin Impact

Margins appear normal but are artificially inflated because shrinkage costs haven't been recognized. True margins are lower than reported.

‚ö†Ô∏è Additional Business Risks

  • Ongoing theft if not addressed
  • Cash flow impact when shrinkage discovered
  • Insurance may not cover undetected shrinkage
  • Employee morale issues if theft is internal
  • Large year-end adjustments at physical inventory

Missing Last Sold Date medium

üìñ Definition

Items without a last sold date cannot be properly aged or analyzed for sales velocity. This could indicate items that have never sold (new products or problems), or it may be a data gap where sales occurred but weren't tracked. Either way, inventory aging analysis is compromised.

‚öôÔ∏è How This Occurs

‚Ä¢ Never sold: New products awaiting first sale ‚Ä¢ Data not tracked: Legacy items added before date tracking ‚Ä¢ System migration: Date field not mapped during conversion ‚Ä¢ Manual sales: Sales processed outside of POS system ‚Ä¢ Data corruption: Date field corrupted or cleared

üí∞ COGS Impact

COGS isn't directly affected, but you can't analyze COGS by product age. Without knowing when items last sold, you can't identify candidates for write-down.

üìà Gross Profit Impact

Cannot determine if items are contributing to profit or just sitting. Profit analysis by product lifecycle stage is impossible.

üìä Gross Margin Impact

Can't correlate margin with inventory age. Items sold at clearance (lower margin) can't be identified from full-price sales.

‚ö†Ô∏è Additional Business Risks

  • Can't identify dead stock without sale date history
  • Inventory aging reports are incomplete
  • May be unknowingly holding obsolete inventory
  • Demand forecasting compromised

Slow-Moving Inventory medium

üìñ Definition

Slow-moving inventory includes items that haven't sold in 6-12 months but aren't yet classified as dead stock. These items are warning signs - they're on the path to becoming obsolete and represent an opportunity for intervention before value is lost. In hardware retail, this is the critical window to take action.

‚öôÔ∏è How This Occurs

‚Ä¢ Demand decline: Product lifecycle entering decline phase ‚Ä¢ Competition: Big box stores or online sellers offering better prices ‚Ä¢ Pricing issues: Price may not match local market expectations ‚Ä¢ Visibility problems: Items buried on bottom shelves or back aisles ‚Ä¢ Seasonal off-cycle: Items awaiting their selling season ‚Ä¢ Customer preference shift: New products replacing older technology ‚Ä¢ Economic factors: Contractors reducing activity in your area

üí∞ COGS Impact

COGS timing is delayed - these items' costs sit on the balance sheet instead of flowing through to expense. While not immediately distorting COGS, the longer items sit, the greater the risk of eventual large write-downs that hit COGS all at once.

üìà Gross Profit Impact

Current gross profit appears normal, but FUTURE gross profit is at risk. When these items eventually sell (likely at discount), the margin will be lower than normal selling prices would generate. The full impact is deferred, not avoided.

üìä Gross Margin Impact

Inventory turn metrics are degraded - money is invested in slow assets vs. fast-turning profitable ones. Your Open-to-Buy is consumed by slow movers instead of generating healthy turns. GMROI (Gross Margin Return on Inventory Investment) suffers.

‚ö†Ô∏è Additional Business Risks

  • Trending toward dead stock and potential complete write-off
  • Tying up Open-to-Buy budget that could fund better items
  • Risk of manufacturer discontinuation increasing daily
  • Packaging deterioration making items less sellable
  • Cash flow constraint: money locked in slow inventory

Overstock / Excess Inventory medium

üìñ Definition

Overstock occurs when you're holding significantly more inventory than needed to meet demand. Measured as months of supply (Quantity on Hand √∑ Monthly Sales Velocity), having more than 12 months of supply typically indicates over-purchasing. Even items that sell can be overstocked if you've bought too many years' worth.

‚öôÔ∏è How This Occurs

‚Ä¢ Bulk discount temptation: Buying more to get better unit pricing from vendors ‚Ä¢ Demand overestimation: Expecting higher sales than materialized ‚Ä¢ Vendor minimum orders: MOQs forcing larger purchases than needed ‚Ä¢ Fear of stockouts: Over-ordering as safety stock ‚Ä¢ Closeout purchases: Buying large quantities of deeply discounted items ‚Ä¢ Seasonal pre-buy gone wrong: Pre-season orders based on optimistic forecasts ‚Ä¢ Automatic reorder errors: Min/max levels set too high

üí∞ COGS Impact

COGS per unit may actually be LOWER due to bulk pricing, but total COGS investment is much higher than necessary. The carrying cost of excess inventory (typically 20-30% of value annually including space, insurance, damage, obsolescence risk) erodes any unit cost savings achieved through bulk buying.

üìà Gross Profit Impact

Gross profit dollars are reduced by carrying costs that often aren't tracked at the SKU level. A product showing 40% gross margin might only deliver 25% after accounting for the cost of capital, storage, and risk of overstock positions.

üìä Gross Margin Impact

Inventory turns are LOW, meaning your gross margin dollars are generated slowly relative to inventory investment. A 50% margin item that turns once per year generates less return than a 30% margin item turning 4x per year.

‚ö†Ô∏è Additional Business Risks

  • Cash flow strain: Working capital tied up unnecessarily
  • Carrying costs: Storage, insurance, handling = 20-30% of value/year
  • Obsolescence risk: Technology or codes may change before sell-through
  • Markdown risk: May need to discount heavily to move excess
  • Opportunity cost: Capital not available for new/better items
  • Warehouse/shelf capacity consumed unproductively
